Virtual Lab — Scaling hands-on learning with cost reduction

How we created an immersive experience for remote nursing education, reducing over R$2 million in operational costs.

Context

Hands-on learning in crisis — and an opportunity for transformation

YDUQS was spending over R$2 million per year on a third-party company to provide practical simulation environments for healthcare courses. The solution was expensive, inflexible, and limited student access to specific schedules and devices.

With the acceleration of remote learning during the pandemic, the limitation became unsustainable: nursing students needed hands-on experience to graduate, but had no access to physical labs. Educational quality was at risk.

The question we received was direct: "Is it possible to build a virtual lab experience in-house, with quality, in 3 months?"

Challenge

Tight deadline, high complexity, massive impact

This wasn't a typical redesign project. It was a strategic bet with simultaneous financial, educational, and operational implications.

3-month deadline

From concept to production delivery. No room for long discovery cycles or extensive iterations. Every decision had to be on target.

Immersive experience

Simulate a real CME (Central Sterile Supply Department) environment with enough fidelity for students to learn real procedures.

Mobile first mandatory

Most distance education students access via mobile. The experience needed to work flawlessly on small screens without losing immersion.

Technical precision

Healthcare demands rigor. Every step of the simulated procedure needed validation by nursing specialists. Error was not an option.

Problem

The cost of external dependency

  • Over R$2 million/year paid to an external vendor for a solution the institution didn't control and couldn't customize.
  • Limited access — students could only use the simulation at specific times and on compatible devices, excluding a large portion of the distance education base.
  • Questionable quality — the third-party solution didn't meet the pedagogical standards that nursing coordinators required.
  • Zero scalability — expanding to other disciplines meant renegotiating contracts and proportionally increasing costs.
  • Total dependency — any change in content or experience depended on the vendor's roadmap, not ours.
Key decisions

Four bets under pressure

01

Mobile first as an access strategy

Decision: Design the entire experience for mobile as the primary platform, not as an adaptation.

Rationale: 70%+ of distance education students access via mobile. Starting with desktop would mean designing for the minority and adapting for the majority.

02

Realistic simulation, not photorealistic

Decision: Focus on functional fidelity (correct procedures, clear feedback) instead of extreme visual fidelity (realistic 3D).

Rationale: The goal is learning, not cinematic immersion. Functional simulation delivers educational value with half the technical effort.

03

Start with the nursing CME

Decision: Choose a specific case (Central Sterile Supply Department) as the MVP instead of trying to cover multiple disciplines.

Rationale: Controlled scope allows delivering with quality in 3 months. Success with the CME validates the model for future expansion.

04

Integrate with the existing Design System

Decision: Build on Factory DS instead of creating components from scratch.

Rationale: Accelerate development and ensure consistency with the YDUQS ecosystem. Less time on UI, more time on experience.

Trade-offs

What we accepted to deliver in 3 months

With an aggressive deadline and ambitious scope, every decision to "not do" was as important as every decision to "do."

Conception

Simulating a real environment on a phone screen

The CME (Central Sterile Supply Department) is a hospital environment where surgical instruments are cleaned, sterilized, and prepared. Nurses must master every step of the process — and errors can have serious consequences.

We created an interactive virtual environment that reproduces the professional's complete journey within the CME: from material reception to release for use. Each step is a guided interaction with immediate feedback on correct and incorrect actions.

The experience was designed to be progressive — the student starts with simple steps and advances to more complex procedures, building confidence before facing the real environment.

Execution

3 months from concept to production

Development was organized in 2-week sprints with continuous validation. Each sprint delivered a functional part of the simulation, tested with real students and reviewed by specialists.

Month 1 — Foundation

Discovery with nursing specialists. CME workflow mapping. Interaction architecture definition. First wireframes and prototypes.

Month 2 — Build

High-fidelity design. Mobile first development. Factory DS integration. Internal testing with product and education teams.

Month 3 — Validation

Testing with nursing students. UI/UX adjustments based on feedback. Final validation with specialists. Production deploy.

Continuous collaboration

Weekly alignment with nursing coordinators, product team, and engineering. Fast decisions, no bureaucracy.

Validation

Tested with the people who truly matter

Validation wasn't a one-time event at the end — it was a continuous process integrated into development.

Student testing

Usability sessions with nursing students at different levels. We identified navigation confusion points and adjusted the information hierarchy.

Specialist review

Every step of the simulated procedure was validated by nursing professionals. Technical precision was non-negotiable.

Iterative adjustments

Feedback turned into improvements each sprint. The final version incorporated over 40 adjustments identified in testing.

Scalability

One model, multiple disciplines

The Virtual Lab was designed from the start as a replicable model, not a one-off solution. The architecture separates the simulation engine from the specific content of each discipline.

This means expanding to new scenarios — pharmacy, physiotherapy, biomedicine — is a matter of creating content, not rebuilding technology. The initial investment pays off exponentially with each new discipline added.

"The CME was the first case. But the architecture was designed so that each new lab costs a fraction of the first — in time and money."

Key takeaways

What this project taught me

  • Constraints drive focus. The 3-month deadline forced quick decisions and eliminated unproductive discussions. Every feature had to justify its existence.
  • Mobile first is strategic in education. It's not a technical decision — it's an access decision. Designing for mobile first is designing for inclusion.
  • Collaboration with specialists increases quality exponentially. Working side by side with nurses transformed the simulation from "beautiful interface" into "real learning tool."
  • A well-defined MVP accelerates impact. Starting with the CME allowed delivering real value in 3 months and validating the model before scaling.
  • Financial impact opens doors. When design generates R$2M+ in savings, the conversation about UX investment changes completely.
